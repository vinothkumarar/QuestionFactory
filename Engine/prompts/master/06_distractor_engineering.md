# PART 6 — DISTRACTOR ENGINEERING

## Objective

Distractors (incorrect options) are educational tools.

They shall not be random.

Every distractor shall represent a realistic mistake that a student
could reasonably make.

Distractors should help diagnose misconceptions.

---

## General Rules

Every multiple-choice question shall contain:

- One correct option.
- Three carefully designed distractors.

Each distractor must be:

- Plausible.
- Educationally meaningful.
- Internally consistent.
- Free from ambiguity.
- Clearly incorrect after proper reasoning.

Never generate absurd or obviously incorrect distractors.

---

## DE-01 — Distractor Categories

Whenever possible, generate distractors from one or more of the
following misconception categories.

### DR01 — Arithmetic Error

Represents simple numerical mistakes.

Examples:

- Incorrect addition
- Incorrect subtraction
- Multiplication mistakes
- Division mistakes

---

### DR02 — Sign Error

Represents mistakes involving positive and negative signs.

Examples:

- Missing negative sign
- Incorrect sign inversion

---

### DR03 — Reciprocal Confusion

Represents incorrect inversion.

Examples:

- x instead of 1/x
- v instead of 1/v

---

### DR04 — Formula Misapplication

Represents selecting the wrong formula or applying a valid formula
outside its domain.

---

### DR05 — Unit Conversion Error

Represents mistakes involving:

- SI conversion
- Prefix conversion
- Incorrect unit substitution

---

### DR06 — Dimensional Error

Represents violations of dimensional consistency.

---

### DR07 — Conceptual Misunderstanding

Represents misunderstanding of the underlying scientific concept.

---

### DR08 — Boundary Condition Error

Represents incorrect treatment of limiting cases or special conditions.

---

### DR09 — Approximation Error

Represents misuse of approximation or rounding.

---

### DR10 — Logical Error

Represents incorrect reasoning despite correct calculations.

---

## DE-02 — Distractor Quality Rules

Every distractor should:

- Reflect a genuine misconception.
- Be believable.
- Be close to the correct answer whenever appropriate.
- Avoid revealing the correct answer.
- Avoid jokes or unrealistic values.

---

## Distractor Distribution

Avoid generating three distractors from the same misconception.

Prefer diversity.

Example:

Option B → Sign Error

Option C → Formula Misapplication

Option D → Unit Conversion Error

---

## Final Verification

Before returning the question verify:

- Exactly one option is correct.
- Every distractor corresponds to a defined misconception.
- Distractors are mutually distinct.
- Distractors do not accidentally become correct.
